Visual / UX

Directly below the executive summary, the UI must immediately answer four questions: What did prototype-creator build? What is working? What needs attention? What is the designer's next action? This decision is about how those four answers are visually presented — the card design, information density, and interaction affordances.

A simple 2x2 grid where each card answers exactly one of the four questions. Each card has a label, a large primary number or short phrase, supporting detail text, and an optional action link. Left-border color indicates severity (green=working, red=attention, blue=action). I recommend this because it's the most direct mapping to the four questions — each card answers one question, no ambiguity.

Works well when

  • Designer needs to answer one specific question quickly
  • Numbers are the primary signal (4/5 pass, 1 fail)
  • Each card can be a click target that scrolls to detail

Watch out for

  • Four equal cards can feel dashboard-y rather than report-like
  • Numbers without context may need the detail text to make sense

A single verdict banner that leads with the overall status (pass/needs review/needs fixes), followed by a compact checklist that answers all four questions inline. Each line is prefixed with a pass/fail/flag icon. The "next action" is a CTA button at the bottom. Reads like a structured summary rather than discrete cards.

Works well when

  • Designer prefers to read rather than parse cards
  • Relationship between items matters (pass/fail story)
  • Fewer visual elements to process

Watch out for

  • Harder to find one specific answer (no card target)
  • Checklist grows linearly with AC count
  • Less visual hierarchy between the four questions

A natural-language paragraph that weaves all four answers into readable prose, followed by an action bar with primary + secondary CTAs. The status badges are inline within the text rather than separate cards. Reads like a human wrote a brief for you, not a machine outputting metrics.

Works well when

  • Context and nuance matter more than raw counts
  • Report should feel like a narrative, not a dashboard
  • The "why" is as important as the "what"

Watch out for

  • Slower to scan than structured cards or checklists
  • Harder to generate consistently — prose varies per eval
  • No visual signal when everything passes vs problems exist

A single dominant metric (the pass rate) takes center stage with a progress bar. Below it, a compact 3-column row provides the remaining answers (built, attention, next action) as small satellite cards. The visual hierarchy is clear — the big number tells you the overall state in one instant, the details are below if you need them.

Works well when

  • One number captures the state (pass rate is the key signal)
  • Strong visual hierarchy — glanceable from across the room
  • Works well at all viewport widths

Watch out for

  • Pass rate alone doesn't capture nuance (flagged ≠ fail)
  • Satellite cards are very small — limited content
  • Feels more like a KPI dashboard than a design review report
